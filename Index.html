• Identify and define your presentation purpose in one sentence before starting.
  • Determine your audience’s knowledge, understanding, and potential problems before preparing content.
  • Incorporate clear definitions for any terms or jargon unfamiliar to your audience.
  • Structure your presentation using the GPS strategy: assess where your audience is, where you want them to go, and how to get them there.
  • Create a strong introduction by grabbing attention, stating a benefit, establishing credibility, and giving direction (ABCD).
  • Use attention getters that are relevant to your topic (facts, questions, or stories).
  • Avoid beginning presentations with personal introductions; instead, start with an effective attention-getter.
  • Visually display all attention-getting questions or facts on your slides.
  • Connect attention-getting openings directly to the problem your presentation addresses.
  • Clearly articulate the main benefit your audience will gain by the end of your introduction.
  • Build credibility efficiently without over-explaining your credentials.
  • Maintain credibility through confident body language, tone, and visual materials.
  • Provide clear direction for the presentation structure in your introduction and throughout the body with transitions and signposting.
  • Utilize ethos, pathos, and logos appeals in your presentation content.
  • Always translate features into clear, audience-oriented benefits.
  • Use visual cues (e.g., progress bars, section slides) to show the audience where you are in the presentation.
  • Practice smooth verbal transitions between topics or sections.
  • Design slides to showcase each major structural element: attention getter, benefit, credibility, and direction.
  • Include a slide for each attention-getting technique and ensure they are visually engaging.
  • Summarize major benefits again during your conclusion (recap).
  • Announce the start of your conclusion section with a clear transition.
  • Offer a specific, actionable call to action and display it visually (consider QR codes or sign-up instructions).
  • Deliver a strong closing statement that circles back to your attention getter and fulfills your opening promise.
  • End presentations with a slide summarizing who you are and how to contact you, but not with "That's all."

Business Communications: Presentation Skills & Structure

Core Concepts of Effective Presentations

Communication Goals

  • Your primary objectives:
  • Get your message received: Eliminate noise and distractions.
  • Ensure understanding: Define unfamiliar terms, avoid jargon your audience doesn’t know.
  • Achieve audience embrace: Especially in persuasive presentations, move the audience to your side (e.g., agreeing, buying, signing up).

"You have to make sure they understand your message, and then make sure they embrace your message." — Instructor Comment

Know Your Audience

  • Audience Profile: Before preparing, assess:
  • What do they already know?
  • What do they not know?
  • What do they value and care about?
  • Implications: Your definitions, examples, and word choices depend on this analysis.

Defining Your Presentation Purpose

  1. Distill your purpose into one sentence.

  1. Clarify for yourself and your audience:
  • What’s their common problem?
  • How will you solve it?

The GPS Strategy

  • G: Where is your audience now? (Current status/understanding/attitude)
  • P: Where do you want them to go? (Your proposed solution/goal)
  • S: What path will get them there? (Persuasive appeals & logical steps)

Presentation Structure (Essay Analogy)

  1. Introduction ("Guess what?")
  2. Body ("So what?")
  3. Conclusion ("Now what?")
  • Bookend Principle: Strong introduction and conclusion are fundamental—they hold everything together.

Introduction ABCDs

Attention

  • Grab attention—first words out of your mouth!
  • Why? You have just 3 seconds to capture the audience. Otherwise, you’ll lose them to distractions.
  • What not to do: Don’t begin with your name, job history, or self-introduction; 90% of presentations do this and it rarely works!

Attention-Getting Techniques:

  1. Facts/Statistics
  • Surprising or relevant facts to stimulate interest.
  • Example: “Did you know malaria may have killed half of all people who ever lived?”
  1. Questions
  • Rhetorical or direct; encourage engagement.
  • Pair with a related fact for impact.
  • Example: “Who wants to make a million dollars legally?”
  1. Stories
  • Brief, memorable anecdotes tied to your message.
  • Stories increase retention and create emotional hooks.
  • Example: Richard Branson’s founding of Virgin Airlines after poor service from a competitor.

"The number one rule in the presentation is that you don't lose your audience."

  • Your attention-getter must be relevant.
  • Avoid unrelated icebreakers or surface-level questions (“Raise your hand if you’ve heard of Amazon…”).
  • Best: Tie attention-getter to a problem your presentation will solve.

Benefit

  • After engaging attention, highlight the main benefit.
  • Why should the audience care?
  • Frame the benefit as a solution to their problem.
  • Tip: Anchor the biggest benefit from your intro, expanding more in the body.

Credibility

  • Show the audience why they should trust you.
  • Offer credentials briefly—don’t overdo your achievements.
  • Example: “I’m a user of this product for 10 years and learned new skills through it.”
  • Methods:
  • Share your role/experience only when relevant.
  • Display confidence, use positive body language.
  • Sprinkle credibility throughout—not just at the start.
  • Dress appropriately for added instant credibility.
  • Credibility mistake: Over-promising then under-delivering destroys trust!

Direction

  • Give a roadmap for the presentation (“Direction”).
  • Tell them what to expect.
  • Use previews: “We’ll cover A, B, and C, ending with Q&A.”
  • Keep them updated during transitions (“Now that we’ve covered X, let’s move to Y.”)
  • Signposting: Use agenda/transition slides and visual cues to orient your audience.
  • Tour Guide Analogy: Never lose your audience—they should always know where they are.

Body of the Presentation: Prove Your Case!

Persuasive Appeals

  1. Ethos (credibility, trustworthiness)
  2. Pathos (emotional connection, story, imagery)
  3. Logos (logic, data, statistics)
  • Feature vs. Benefit:
  • Feature: What something is (e.g., 1 GB of storage).
  • Benefit: What it means for the audience (“1,000 songs in your pocket.”)
  • Continually link back to the main problem and solution/benefit.
  • Use visual cues (headers, markers, agenda slides) to show progression.

Conclusion Structure (ABCD)

Announce

  • Signal you’re wrapping up with a clear transition (“In conclusion…” or visual cue).
  • Prevents your audience from feeling lost as you close.

Benefit (Recap)

  • Restate the main benefit(s) from intro and body.
  • Reinforce what’s in it for the audience.

Call to Action (CTA)

  • Directly state what you want your audience to do.
  • Use persuasive principles like urgency or social proof (e.g., “Sign up now—spots are limited!”).
  • QR codes, testimonials, discounts, or stats can be effective.
  • Be confident. Avoid language like “I hope you might consider…”

Deliver (Strong Ending)

  • Come full circle—connect your ending to your original attention-getter.
  • Repeat fact/question/story from your opening for closure.
  • Leave a strong, memorable statement or quote.
  • End on your own terms: avoid “That’s it… so, yeah…”—memorize your final line.
  • Finish with a “Thank you,” and let the applause begin.

“Your delivery needs to tie back to that attention getter.” — Instructor

Presentation Slide Design Tips

  • Slides should support—not distract from—your speech.
  • Have a dedicated slide for each ABCD element:
  • Attention (Fact/Question/Story)—display the key text/image.
  • Benefit—bold, concise summary.
  • Credibility—team/company intro with images/names.
  • Direction—visual agenda/roadmap.
  • Announce/transition—visual cue that the conclusion has started.
  • CTA—clear, with helpful tools (QR, brief instructions).
  • Delivery—closing thought or quote.
  • Less is more: Minimize text, use bold visuals and narrative images.
  • Credible visuals: Use high-quality images, and cite any data sources if appropriate.

Best Practices & Key Takeaways

  • Memorize your opening, closing, and transitions—these are most vital.
  • Audience retention hinges on strong introductions and conclusions.
  • Never start with your bio—begin by engaging the audience.
  • Be the confident tour guide—never let your audience get lost.
  • Strong, clear, enthusiastic delivery builds trust and drives action.
  • Tie everything back to the audience’s needs and your core message.
